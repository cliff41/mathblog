Forgetting to Reverse Symbols

This is a mistake I frequently make and my teacher never truly explained this. I kept on getting questions wrong and wondered, "What on Earth did I do wrong?"

As an example, let's take this question from Cambridge Maths NSW Year 9 5.3 Second Edition. This is Chapter 8D, question 7g.



Image Text:
Factorise the following:
g) 49-(𝑥+3)2

First, as the example in the textbook suggests, turn the 49 into a square, so we can make the equation a difference of squares.
= 72-(𝑥+3)2

We all know that the expansion of the difference of squares is (𝑎+𝑏)(𝑎-𝑏), with in this case 𝑎 being 72 and 𝑏 being -(𝑥+3)2. This is the next step, however here I made a mistake.

I got the answer (7+𝑥+3)(7-𝑥+3). I thought this was right. One of 𝑥+3's was subtracting from 7, the other one adding. "That follows the rules, right?" is what I thought in my head.
I added the 7 and 3 in both brackets, with a final answer of: (10+x)(10-x). After I finish the entirety of the excersise, I flip back to the answers. Low and behold, I got 7g wrong.

The answer was… (10 + x)(4 – x)? I thought this wrong. I chucked it into Microsoft Math Solver. Same answer (although it had used a different method from me and the textbook). I hopped on the online version of the textbook, with its step-by-step answers. I was stunned.

Remember that step where I got (7+𝑥+3)(7-𝑥+3)? Well, it turns out they got (7+𝑥+3)(7-𝑥-3). "How???" I shouted. I almost woke up all my neighbours with my 3am math shenanigans.



Now, time for the math explanation. If you skipped to this part, duck you.

I got the first set of brackets correct, which required adding. However, it was the subtracting I got wrong. 7-(𝑥+3). I got that simple of an equation wrong? Dumbfounded, I consulted Bing AI. It used a different method to solve the equation, meaning it took me some time to fully understand its response.

The AI said to me that you have to change the signs of the 𝑥 and the 3, which means changing them from positive to negative. The reason why is because when you subtract 𝑥+3, what you are really doing is subtracting each of the terms individually from 7. If that's a little complicated, then just imagine that the positive 𝑥 and positive 3 turned into negatives because the minus sign beside the brackets was being multiplied with each term.

So remember: If you've got to subtract a positive binomial from something, remember to turn all the terms in the binomial negative.
